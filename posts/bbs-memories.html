My Favorite BBS Memories

Published: October 10, 2025 Category: Nostalgia By: Webmaster

Before the web as we know it, there were Bulletin Board Systems (BBS). Here are some of my fondest memories of dialing up to these digital communities in the early 90s.

What Was a BBS?

For those who weren't around in the pre-internet era, a Bulletin Board System (BBS) was a computer server running software that allowed users to connect to the system using a terminal program. Once connected, the user could perform functions such as downloading software, reading news and bulletins, exchanging messages with other users through email and public message boards, and sometimes chatting with other users in real-time.

Unlike the modern internet where you can visit websites all over the world simultaneously, BBS users would connect directly to a specific system, one at a time, often using their telephone line and a modem. This meant you'd have to disconnect from one BBS to call another, and your phone line would be busy the entire time you were connected.

My First BBS Experience

I'll never forget my first BBS experience. It was 1992, and I had just gotten a 2400 baud modem for my computer. A friend at school had given me a list of local BBS phone numbers, and I spent an entire Saturday afternoon trying to connect to them.

After several failed attempts (busy signals were common since most BBSes had only one phone line), I finally connected to "The Digital Dungeon," a fantasy-themed BBS run by someone who called himself "The Wizard." The text-based interface appeared on my screen, welcoming me to this new digital realm with an ASCII art dragon.

                |\    /|
                (o\  /o)
                 ( >< )
                 / .. \
   ______________\_\/_/______________
  /                                  \
 |    Welcome to The Digital Dungeon   |
 |      Where Fantasy Meets Tech       |
  \__________________________________/

I spent hours exploring message boards, downloading shareware games, and even chatting with The Wizard himself, who turned out to be a 40-year-old computer programmer who ran the BBS as a hobby. It was my first taste of online community, and I was hooked.

Favorite BBS Games

One of the best things about BBSes were the door games - games that ran on the BBS server that multiple users could play, albeit usually not simultaneously. Here were some of my favorites:

1. Trade Wars 2002

This space trading and combat game was the pinnacle of BBS gaming. Players would explore the universe, trade commodities between planets, build their fleet, and engage in combat with other players. The game persisted between sessions, so you'd log in each day to see how your empire had fared.

2. Legend of the Red Dragon (LORD)

A fantasy role-playing game where you would fight monsters, flirt with barmaids, and challenge other players to duels. Each day you'd get a certain number of "forest fights" to build up your character. The social aspects were just as important as the combat.

3. BRE (Barren Realms Elite)

A strategy game where you'd build and manage a kingdom, balancing resources like food, land, and money while engaging in diplomacy and warfare with other players.

4. Global War

A Risk-like game where players conquered territories on a global map. Alliances and betrayals were common, making it as much a social experiment as a game.

The Thrill of File Sections

Before app stores and websites like GitHub, BBS file sections were the primary way to discover and download software. Each download was an adventure:

  1. Finding something interesting - BBSes had file listings with descriptions, but they were often vague or hyperbolic.
  2. The download process - Starting a download and watching as the estimated time slowly (very slowly) counted down. A 1MB file could take 30+ minutes at 2400 baud.
  3. The risk factor - Would the file be what was described? Would it contain a virus? Would your mom pick up the phone mid-download and ruin everything?
  4. The payoff - Successfully downloading and running a new game or useful utility felt like genuine achievement.

I remember downloading a game called "Wolfenstein 3D" from a local BBS in 1993. It took over an hour, and I had to ask my family not to use the phone. When it finally finished and I ran the game, I was blown away by the 3D graphics. It was unlike anything I'd ever seen before.

The Social Aspect

What I miss most about the BBS era was the sense of community. Each BBS had its own culture, inside jokes, and regular users. Some of my fondest memories include:

  • Message boards - Discussions ranged from technical support to philosophical debates to teenage drama.
  • SysOps - The System Operators were like local celebrities who set the tone for their BBS.
  • Meet-ups - Some larger BBSes would organize real-life gatherings, where you'd finally meet the people behind the usernames.
  • File validation - Getting "validated" by a SysOp to gain access to more download slots or the elite file sections felt like being admitted to a secret club.

The End of an Era

As the internet became more accessible in the mid-90s, BBSes began to fade away. Many tried to adapt by offering internet email gateways or web access, but the writing was on the wall. By 1996, most of the BBSes I frequented had shut down.

The last BBS I regularly called was "The Outer Limits," which finally closed in 1997 when the SysOp posted a farewell message announcing he was moving the community to a web forum instead.

While the internet brought amazing new possibilities, something special was lost in the transition - the local, intimate nature of BBSes, the sense of place they provided in the digital realm, and the deeply personal connection between users and system operators.

Modern BBS Revival

Interestingly, there's been a small but dedicated revival of the BBS scene in recent years. Some enthusiasts run BBSes that can be accessed via telnet instead of dial-up modems. These systems preserve the look and feel of classic BBSes, including ANSI art, door games, and message boards.

If you're curious to experience this piece of computing history, check out sites like:

  • Level 29: A modern BBS with classic features
  • Particles BBS: Known for its extensive door game collection
  • SynchronetBBS: Both software for running your own BBS and a list of active boards

Conclusion

The BBS era represents a unique moment in computing history - a time when online communities were small, local, and deeply personal. While today's internet offers incredible connectivity and resources, I sometimes miss the charm and intimacy of connecting to a local BBS, hearing the modem handshake, and entering a digital world that felt like a secret clubhouse rather than a global marketplace.

Do you have BBS memories to share? Did you run your own board? Let me know in the comments or sign my guestbook!
